The Babylonian Method

Q: What is \(\sqrt{5}\)? By definition, it solves \(x^2-5=0\). But how can we compute this number?

Make an initial guess for the square root. For example, \(x_0=2.5\).

Too big, since \(2.5^2=6.25\). Then \(5/2.5\) is too small.

This suggests a way to improve upon the initial guess: average your number that is too big and your number that is too small.

\[ x_1=\frac12\left(2.5+\frac{5}{2.5}\right)=2.25. \]

Repeat.

\[ x_2=\frac12\left(2.25+\frac{5}{2.25}\right) =2.236\overline{1}. \]

One could keep going, but this is already so close. \(2.236\overline{1}\) is accurate for \(\sqrt5\) to three decimal places!

\[ \sqrt5\approx 2.236 \]

Without a calculator? Note that in this example, we could have used fractions throughout:

\[ x_0=\frac52,\qquad x_1=\frac94,\qquad x_2=\frac{161}{72},\ldots \]

Then we get rational approximations of the square root, calculator-free.

This ancient method can be understood on its own—it is fun to think about why it works. Or it can be viewed as a special case of Newton's method. Newton's method is for approximating zeros of functions and uses the derivative. In this example, the function would be \(f(x)=x^2-5\). Newton's method is the content of Section 4.8 in your Calculus book.
